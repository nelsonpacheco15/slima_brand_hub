## THREADS (text-ready)

**T1 — truth / relatability**
> Your writing group lied to you.
> Not on purpose. They just like you more than they like being honest.
> The only people who will tell you the truth about your book are the ones who don't know you. And they already stopped reading.

**T2 — hot take**
> Most books don't fail because they're badly written.
> They fail because the writer never found out where readers got bored, until the one-star reviews showed up.

**T3 — list (saveable)**
> Things that quietly kill a first novel:
> - a chapter 1 that's all backstory
> - a middle where nothing happens
> - a character whose eyes change color between chapters
> - you, deciding to start over for the fourth time
> Every one of them is fixable, if you can see it.

**T4 — POV (self-pub + beginner)**
> POV: you spent a year on your novel, published it, and the top review says "I put it down around chapter 3."
> That review existed the whole time. You just couldn't read it until it was too late.

**T5 — research / PhD niche**
> Nobody doing a PhD is struggling with writing.
> They're struggling with 40 files, 6 open tabs, and a brain that remembers the perfect quote but not where it lives.
> Fix the finding and the writing gets weirdly easy.

**T6 — method / 80/20**
> The 80/20 rule for finishing your book:
> you lose about 80% of readers in 20% of the pages.
> So stop rewriting the whole thing. Find those pages. Fix those pages. Ship the book.

**T7 — encouragement (quotable)**
> A bad draft is a book. A perfect idea in your head is not.
> You can edit 50,000 messy words. You cannot edit zero.

**T8 — question (begs replies)**
> Writers: what's the chapter you always get stuck on?
> Mine is the one right after the exciting opening, when I actually have to make things happen.
> There's a reason it's always the same spot.

**T9 — vs. your tools (high intent)**
> Writing your novel in Notion is like keeping a hospital's records in a really nice notebook.
> Organized. Pretty. No idea whether the patient is okay.
> Your manuscript needs a tool that actually reads it.

**T10 — validation wedge**
> You do not need 1,500 dollars for an editor to find out if your book works.
> You need one honest reader who finishes the whole thing and tells you the truth.
> That used to take weeks. It doesn't have to anymore.
