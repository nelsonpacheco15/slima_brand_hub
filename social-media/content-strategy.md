# Slima — Social Content Strategy (Instagram + Threads)

The plan for growing Slima's organic reach on Threads (text-first) and Instagram (visual), built on the brand voice (`../brand/01-brand-guidelines.md`), copywriting method, and marketing psychology. Every post obeys the hard rules: **never lead with "AI", no em-dashes, plain language, no "chapter-N" trope, warm and literary.**

Platforms share the **same handle** (Threads inherits Instagram). One brand, two formats:
- **Threads** = short, honest, conversational takes. Value or relatability first, product second. This is where organic reach compounds fastest.
- **Instagram** = the editorial line-illustration posts + carousels (teaching) + short feature clips. Visual proof.

## 5. Example posts (2 per pillar) — Threads copy + IG treatment

> Threads copy is ready to post (no em-dashes). IG = the visual treatment.

### Pillar 1 — The honest truth
**A.**
> "It's really good!" is what people say when they don't want to hurt you.
> Your mom. Your writing group. The friend who "read it."
> None of them will tell you the chapter where they quietly gave up.
> That's the one you need to fix.
*(IG: the "Your friends won't tell you the truth" line illustration.)*

**B.**
> Nobody tells you they stopped reading. They just stop.
> No note, no reason, no closure. Just a book left open on page 60 forever.
> Wouldn't you rather know where, while you can still fix it?

### Pillar 2 — Craft & methods
**A. (80/20)**
> The 80/20 rule for finishing your book:
> 80% of the readers you lose, you lose in about 20% of the pages.
> You don't have to rewrite the whole thing. You have to find those pages.
> An honest read shows you exactly where they are.
*(IG carousel: "The 80/20 rule for your novel", 5 slides.)*

**B. (Second Brain)**
> Your novel isn't 1 file. It's 40.
> Character notes. The timeline you swear you'll clean up. Three chapter drafts. Research tabs you never closed.
> A second brain for writers isn't fancy. It's one place that remembers all of it, so you don't have to.

### Pillar 3 — Second brain / research
**A.**
> Writing a PhD or a nonfiction book?
> Your problem was never the writing. It's that your research lives in 40 files, 3 apps, and one very tired brain.
> Put the whole project in one place that remembers it. Then write.

**B.**
> The most underrated research skill: being able to find what you already read.
> Notes are worthless if you can't reach them at the exact moment you're writing the paragraph they belong to.

### Pillar 4 — Show, don't tell  *(needs screen recording — see §6)*
**A.**
> We built a reader that tells you the truth.
> It reads your chapter and draws a curve: where attention holds, where it drops, and the line people would close the book.
> [15s screen recording: attention curve with the drop marked]

**B.**
> Blue eyes in chapter 2. Grey eyes in chapter 19.
> Slima catches it before a reviewer does.
> [screen recording: continuity check, green check turning to amber flag]

### Pillar 5 — Validation without the cost  (the wedge)
**A.**
> Beta readers: 3 weeks, if they finish.
> A developmental editor: 1,500 dollars and up.
> One-star reviews for a problem you could have caught: free, and much worse.
> There is now a way to get an honest read in minutes, before any of that.

**B.**
> You don't need to spend a fortune to find out if your book works.
> You need one honest reader who finishes it and tells you the truth. Today, not in a month.

### Pillar 6 — Writer wins & milestones
**A.**
> 50,000 words.
> That is not "a lot of writing." That is a novel.
> If you are anywhere on the way there, you are further than almost everyone who only ever talks about writing one.
*(IG: quote card, IBM Plex Serif, indigo accent.)*

**B.**
> The draft doesn't have to be good. It has to exist.
> You can fix a page. You cannot fix a blank one.

### Pillar 7 — vs. the tools you use now
**A.**
> Writing your book in Notion?
> Notion organizes your notes. It does not know your story.
> Slima reads the whole manuscript and keeps it straight. Different job, different tool.

**B.**
> ChatGPT forgets your book a few pages in. Word just types.
> If you are writing something long, you want a tool built for the book, not the message.
