## H1 — scene fix
- **Post:** A flat scene is almost always missing one of three things.
- **Comment 1:** Someone wanting something. Something in their way. Something different by the end.
- **Comment 2:** Quick test: summarize the scene as "___ tries to ___ but ___." If you can't fill all three blanks, it's the scene that's broken, not the prose.
- **Comment 3:** Slima reads your whole book and points to the scenes readers slow down in, so you know which to check first. Link in bio.

## H2 — revision order
- **Post:** Most people revise a book in the wrong order.
- **Comment 1:** Do it like this. 1. Structure: are the right scenes even here? 2. Scenes: does each one move things forward? 3. Lines: is it clean?
- **Comment 2:** Start at 1, not 3. Never polish a sentence in a scene you might cut.
- **Comment 3:** Slima helps with step 1. It shows you where the structure loses readers before you rewrite anything. Link in bio.

## H3 — reverse outline
- **Post:** There's a ten-minute exercise that tells you exactly which scenes to cut.
- **Comment 1:** After you finish a draft, write one line per scene: what changes in it. The scenes where nothing changes are the ones readers skip.
- **Comment 2:** Do it in a spreadsheet: one row per scene, one column for "what changes." The empty cells are your cut list.
- **Comment 3:** Slima does this read for you and shows where attention actually drops off. Link in bio.

## H4 — better feedback
- **Post:** The most useful thing you can ask a reader isn't "did you like it?"
- **Comment 1:** It's "where did you put it down and do something else?" That one answer is worth more than a hundred compliments.
- **Comment 2:** Ask three readers that question. The chapter they all name is your first fix.
- **Comment 3:** Or skip the wait. Slima reads your book and marks the exact spot readers lose interest. Link in bio.

## H5 — research system (PhD / nonfiction)
- **Post:** The reason research takes forever isn't the reading.
- **Comment 1:** It's that you can never find the quote you need when you need it.
- **Comment 2:** Fix it: one note per source, in your own words, with the page number. The "own words" part matters most. Copy-paste and you'll re-read it later confused.
- **Comment 3:** Slima keeps every source in one place and remembers them while you write. Link in bio.

## H6 — character continuity
- **Post:** Most continuity mistakes come from one small thing.
- **Comment 1:** A forgotten physical detail. Keep one line per character: what they want, what they're hiding, one detail you'll otherwise lose.
- **Comment 2:** Pick a detail you'll actually reference: a scar, a habit, the way they take their coffee.
- **Comment 3:** Slima remembers every character across the whole book and tells you when a detail changes. Link in bio.

## H7 — when to write
- **Post:** If writing feels impossible at night, it's not you. It's the order of your day.
- **Comment 1:** Writing is decisions, and you've spent all day making them. Do your pages first, before the tank is empty.
- **Comment 2:** Even twenty minutes before you open email counts. Protect the first block, not the last one.
- **Comment 3:** Slima keeps your whole story in front of you, so fewer decisions start from scratch. Link in bio.

## H8 — getting started
- **Post:** The blank page isn't a discipline problem. It's a standards problem.
- **Comment 1:** You can't start because you're trying to start well. Write it badly for twenty minutes and let it be bad.
- **Comment 2:** Start mid-scene, not at the beginning. Openings are the hardest thing to write, so write them last.
- **Comment 3:** And when you come back tomorrow, Slima remembers exactly where you left off. Link in bio.

## H9 — finishing
- **Post:** Books don't get finished on the big writing days.
- **Comment 1:** They get finished on the small ones. Five hundred words when you don't feel like it. Those are the ones that add up.
- **Comment 2:** Lower the bar until you can't say no. "One sentence" days keep the streak alive, and most turn into more.
- **Comment 3:** Slima keeps every draft and every day's work, so nothing you write gets lost. Link in bio.

## H10 — self-publishing
- **Post:** There's a free edit that takes ten minutes and catches most of your worst lines.
- **Comment 1:** Read your first page out loud. Every place you stumble is a place a reader will too.
- **Comment 2:** Even better, have your phone read it back to you. Your ear catches what your eye skips.
- **Comment 3:** For the rest of the book, Slima gives you an honest read before you publish. Link in bio.

## H11 — reader attention
- **Post:** Readers never quit where you think they do.
- **Comment 1:** Not at the boring part. A couple pages later, when they realize it isn't picking up. That's why you can't feel it while writing.
- **Comment 2:** "Trust your gut" fails on your own book. You know it picks up, so you can't feel the drag. You need an outside read.
- **Comment 3:** Slima gives you one. It shows the exact spot readers start to drift. Link in bio.

## H12 — capture research early
- **Post:** The most expensive habit in research is "I'll organize it later."
- **Comment 1:** Later is where good sources go to be forgotten. Organize each one the moment you find it.
- **Comment 2:** Two minutes now saves an hour later. Note the source, the point, and where you'll use it, before you close the tab.
- **Comment 3:** Slima captures it and remembers it for you, so "later" isn't a problem. Link in bio.
