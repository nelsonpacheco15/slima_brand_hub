# Helpful Threads posts (hook first, then value, then Slima)

Line 1 is the hook. On Threads it's what shows in the feed and decides whether people stop scrolling, so it has to stop the scroll and promise a payoff. Then the tip. Then one plain Slima line. Still Notion voice: plain, human, no clickbait. No "manuscript," no antithesis, no analogies, no em-dashes. Account: **@writewithslima**.

**H1 — scene fix**
> A flat scene is almost always missing one of three things.
> 1. Someone wanting something.
> 2. Something in their way.
> 3. Something different by the end.
> No want, no obstacle, no change. That's the scene to rewrite.
> Slima reads your book and points to the scenes readers slow down in.

**H2 — revision order**
> Most people revise a book in the wrong order.
> Do it like this:
> 1. Structure. Are the right scenes even here?
> 2. Scenes. Does each one move things forward?
> 3. Lines. Is it clean?
> Start at 1, not 3. That's why the line edits never fixed it.
> Slima helps with step 1. It shows you where the structure loses people, before you touch a line.

**H3 — reverse outline**
> There's a ten-minute exercise that tells you exactly which scenes to cut.
> After you finish a draft, write one line per scene: what changes in it.
> The scenes where nothing changes are the ones readers skip.
> Slima can find them for you. It reads the whole book and shows where attention drops.

**H4 — better feedback**
> The most useful thing you can ask a reader isn't "did you like it?"
> It's "where did you put it down and do something else?"
> That one answer is worth more than a hundred compliments.
> Slima answers it for you. It marks the exact spot readers lose interest.

**H5 — research system (PhD / nonfiction)**
> The reason research takes forever isn't the reading.
> It's that you can never find the quote you need when you need it.
> Fix it: one note per source, in your own words, with the page number. Then you're arranging notes, not re-reading forty PDFs.
> Slima keeps every source in one place and remembers them while you write.

**H6 — character continuity**
> Most continuity mistakes come from one small thing: a forgotten physical detail.
> Keep one line per character: what they want, what they're hiding, one detail you'll otherwise lose.
> Slima remembers every character across the whole book, and flags it when a detail changes.

**H7 — when to write**
> If writing feels impossible at night, it's not you. It's the order of your day.
> Writing is decisions, and you've spent all day making them. Do your pages first, before the tank is empty.
> Slima keeps your whole story in front of you, so fewer of those decisions start from scratch.

**H8 — getting started**
> The blank page isn't a discipline problem. It's a standards problem.
> You can't start because you're trying to start well. Write it badly for twenty minutes and let it be bad.
> And when you come back tomorrow, Slima remembers exactly where you were.

**H9 — finishing**
> Books don't get finished on the big writing days.
> They get finished on the small ones. Five hundred words when you don't feel like it. Those are the ones that add up.
> Slima keeps every draft and every day's work. Nothing you write gets lost.

**H10 — self-publishing**
> There's a free edit that takes ten minutes and catches most of your worst lines.
> Read your first page out loud. Every place you stumble is a place a reader will too.
> For the rest of the book, Slima gives you an honest read before you publish.

**H11 — reader attention**
> Readers never quit where you think they do.
> Not at the boring part. A couple pages later, when they realize it isn't picking up. That's why you can't feel it while writing.
> Slima can. It shows you the exact spot readers start to drift.

**H12 — capture research early**
> The most expensive habit in research is "I'll organize it later."
> Later is where good sources go to be forgotten. Organize each one the moment you find it.
> Slima captures it and remembers it for you, so later isn't a problem.

---
**Cadence:** 4–5 a week, mixed with `posts-batch-01.md`. Lead with H4, H3, H11, H1 (strongest hooks). If a hook underperforms, swap line 1 first, keep the body.
