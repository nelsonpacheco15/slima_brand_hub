Links live in the comment so the post's reach isn't suppressed. Notion voice throughout. Account: **@writewithslima**. Link = in bio (app.slima.ai).

**H1 — scene fix**
- **Post:** A flat scene is almost always missing one of three things. Someone wanting something. Something in their way. Something different by the end. No want, no obstacle, no change. That's the scene to rewrite.
- **Pinned comment:** Quick test: summarize the scene as "___ tries to ___ but ___." If you can't fill all three blanks, it's the scene that's broken, not the prose. Slima reads your whole book and flags the scenes readers slow down in, so you know which ones to check first. Link in bio.

**H2 — revision order**
- **Post:** Most people revise a book in the wrong order. Do it like this: 1. Structure. Are the right scenes even here? 2. Scenes. Does each one move things forward? 3. Lines. Is it clean? Start at 1, not 3. That's why the line edits never fixed it.
- **Pinned comment:** Rule of thumb: never polish a sentence in a scene you might cut. Finish structure and scene work first. Slima helps with step 1. It shows you where the structure loses readers before you rewrite anything. Link in bio.

**H3 — reverse outline**
- **Post:** There's a ten-minute exercise that tells you exactly which scenes to cut. After you finish a draft, write one line per scene: what changes in it. The scenes where nothing changes are the ones readers skip.
- **Pinned comment:** Do it in a spreadsheet: one row per scene, one column for "what changes." The empty cells are your cut list. Slima does this read for you and shows where attention actually drops off. Link in bio.

**H4 — better feedback**
- **Post:** The most useful thing you can ask a reader isn't "did you like it?" It's "where did you put it down and do something else?" That one answer is worth more than a hundred compliments.
- **Pinned comment:** Ask three readers that one question. The chapter they all name is your first fix. Or skip the wait: Slima reads your book and marks the exact spot readers lose interest. Link in bio.

**H5 — research system (PhD / nonfiction)**
- **Post:** The reason research takes forever isn't the reading. It's that you can never find the quote you need when you need it. Fix it: one note per source, in your own words, with the page number.
- **Pinned comment:** The "in your own words" part matters most. Copy-paste and you'll re-read it later trying to understand it. Summarize once, use it forever. Slima keeps every source in one place and remembers them while you write. Link in bio.

**H6 — character continuity**
- **Post:** Most continuity mistakes come from one small thing: a forgotten physical detail. Keep one line per character: what they want, what they're hiding, one detail you'll otherwise lose.
- **Pinned comment:** Pick a detail you'll actually reference: a scar, a habit, the way they take their coffee. Slima remembers every character across the whole book and flags it when a detail changes. Link in bio.

**H7 — when to write**
- **Post:** If writing feels impossible at night, it's not you. It's the order of your day. Writing is decisions, and you've spent all day making them. Do your pages first, before the tank is empty.
- **Pinned comment:** Even 20 minutes before you open email counts. Protect the first block, not the last one. Slima keeps your whole story in front of you, so you spend fewer decisions just re-finding where you were. Link in bio.

**H8 — getting started**
- **Post:** The blank page isn't a discipline problem. It's a standards problem. You can't start because you're trying to start well. Write it badly for twenty minutes and let it be bad.
- **Pinned comment:** Trick: start mid-scene, not at the beginning. Openings are the hardest thing to write, so write them last. And when you come back tomorrow, Slima remembers exactly where you left off. Link in bio.

**H9 — finishing**
- **Post:** Books don't get finished on the big writing days. They get finished on the small ones. Five hundred words when you don't feel like it. Those are the ones that add up.
- **Pinned comment:** Lower the bar until you can't say no. "One sentence" days keep the streak alive, and most turn into more. Slima keeps every draft and every day's work, so nothing you write gets lost. Link in bio.

**H10 — self-publishing**
- **Post:** There's a free edit that takes ten minutes and catches most of your worst lines. Read your first page out loud. Every place you stumble is a place a reader will too.
- **Pinned comment:** Even better, have your phone read it back to you. Your ear catches what your eye skips. For the rest of the book, Slima gives you an honest read before you publish. Link in bio.

**H11 — reader attention**
- **Post:** Readers never quit where you think they do. Not at the boring part. A couple pages later, when they realize it isn't picking up. That's why you can't feel it while writing.
- **Pinned comment:** This is why "trust your gut" fails on your own book. You know it picks up, so you can't feel the drag. You need an outside read. Slima gives you one: it shows the exact spot readers start to drift. Link in bio.

**H12 — capture research early**
- **Post:** The most expensive habit in research is "I'll organize it later." Later is where good sources go to be forgotten. Organize each one the moment you find it.
- **Pinned comment:** Two minutes now saves an hour later. Note the source, the point, and where you'll use it, before you close the tab. Slima captures it and remembers it for you, so "later" isn't a problem. Link in bio.
