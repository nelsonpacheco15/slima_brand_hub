## H1 — scene fix
- **Post:** A flat scene is almost always missing one of three things.
- **Comment 1:** Someone wanting something. Something in their way. Something different by the end.
- **Comment 2:** Quick test: summarize the scene as "___ tries to ___ but ___." If all three blanks fill in, the scene works. If they don't, that's the one to rewrite.
- **Comment 3:** Slima reads your whole book and points to the scenes readers slow down in, so you know which to check first. Link in bio.

## H2 — revision order
- **Post:** Most people revise a book in the wrong order.
- **Comment 1:** Do it like this. 1. Structure: are the right scenes even here? 2. Scenes: does each one move things forward? 3. Lines: is it clean?
- **Comment 2:** Start at 1. Save the sentence polishing for scenes you know you're keeping.
- **Comment 3:** Slima helps with step 1. It shows you where the structure loses readers before you rewrite anything. Link in bio.

## H3 — reverse outline
- **Post:** There's a ten-minute exercise that tells you exactly which scenes to cut.
- **Comment 1:** After you finish a draft, write one line per scene: what changes in it. The scenes where nothing changes are the ones readers skip.
- **Comment 2:** Do it in a spreadsheet: one row per scene, one column for "what changes." The empty cells are your cut list.
- **Comment 3:** Slima does this read for you and shows where attention actually drops off. Link in bio.

## H4 — better feedback
- **Post:** The most useful question to ask a reader: where did you put it down and do something else?
- **Comment 1:** That one answer is worth more than a hundred compliments.
- **Comment 2:** Ask three readers the same question. The chapter they all name is your first fix.
- **Comment 3:** Slima can point you there too. It reads your book and marks the exact spot readers lose interest. Link in bio.

## H5 — research system (PhD / nonfiction)
- **Post:** Somewhere in your forty files is the exact quote you need right now.
- **Comment 1:** That's where the hours go. You read it once, then spend an afternoon hunting for it again.
- **Comment 2:** Fix it: one note per source, in your own words, with the page number. Summarize it once and you'll actually understand it later.
- **Comment 3:** Slima keeps every source in one place and remembers them while you write. Link in bio.

## H6 — character continuity
- **Post:** Your main character had blue eyes in chapter 2. By chapter 19 they're grey.
- **Comment 1:** These little details drift in every long project. A name spelling shifts, a Tuesday becomes a Thursday, a scar moves cheeks.
- **Comment 2:** Keep one line per character: what they want, what they're hiding, one physical detail you'll otherwise lose. Check it before you publish.
- **Comment 3:** Slima remembers every character across the whole book and tells you when a detail changes. Link in bio.

## H7 — when to write
- **Post:** If writing feels impossible at night, it's the order of your day.
- **Comment 1:** Writing is decisions, and you've spent all day making them. Do your pages first, while you still have some left.
- **Comment 2:** Even twenty minutes before you open email counts. Make it the first thing you do.
- **Comment 3:** Slima keeps your whole story in front of you, so more of those decisions are already made. Link in bio.

## H8 — getting started
- **Post:** Blank-page paralysis usually comes down to one thing: you're trying to start it well.
- **Comment 1:** Give yourself twenty minutes to write it badly on purpose. The rough version is the hard part to get past.
- **Comment 2:** Start mid-scene. Openings are the hardest thing to write, so save them for last.
- **Comment 3:** And when you come back tomorrow, Slima remembers exactly where you left off. Link in bio.

## H9 — finishing
- **Post:** Books get finished on the small writing days.
- **Comment 1:** The five hundred words you drag out on a tired day. Those are the ones that add up.
- **Comment 2:** Lower the bar until it's easy to say yes. A one-sentence day keeps the streak alive, and most turn into more.
- **Comment 3:** Slima keeps every draft and every day's work. Everything you write stays. Link in bio.

## H10 — self-publishing
- **Post:** There's a free edit that takes ten minutes and catches most of your worst lines.
- **Comment 1:** Read your first page out loud. Every place you stumble is a place a reader will too.
- **Comment 2:** Even better, have your phone read it back to you. Your ear catches what your eye skips.
- **Comment 3:** For the rest of the book, Slima gives you an honest read before you publish. Link in bio.

## H11 — reader attention
- **Post:** Readers quit a page or two after the part you'd expect.
- **Comment 1:** They give the slow stretch a chance to pick up. When it stays slow, they're gone. That's why it's so hard to spot in your own book.
- **Comment 2:** You already know it picks up, so you read right past the drag. An outside read sees it clearly.
- **Comment 3:** Slima gives you that read. It shows the exact spot readers start to drift. Link in bio.

## H12 — capture research early
- **Post:** The most expensive habit in research is "I'll organize it later."
- **Comment 1:** Later is where good sources go to be forgotten. Organize each one the moment you find it.
- **Comment 2:** Two minutes now saves an hour later. Note the source, the point, and where you'll use it, before you close the tab.
- **Comment 3:** Slima captures it and remembers it for you, so "later" stops being a problem. Link in bio.
