INSIGHTS A long read on finishing

Why 97 of every 100 books never get finished.

S The Slima team · June 18, 2026 · 9 min read

Almost everyone who starts a long book stops somewhere in the middle. Not because the idea was bad, and not because they ran out of talent. They lose the thread. By chapter twelve they can no longer hold the whole shape of the thing in their head, and the draft quietly becomes a folder they stop opening.

This is the single most common way a book dies. It is worth understanding, because the fix is not "try harder." It is structural.

Holding a whole book is too much for working memory

Your working memory can hold about four things at once. A novel has hundreds: characters, timelines, a promise you made in chapter two, a name you changed in chapter seven. When the book outgrows your head, every writing session starts with the same tax. You reread, you re-orient, you try to remember what you already decided.

That tax is where momentum dies. The writers who finish are not the ones with better memories. They are the ones who stopped relying on memory at all.

The finishers are not more disciplined. They just stopped carrying the whole book in their head.

Three habits the finishers share

When you look at people who actually reach the end of a long draft, the same patterns show up again and again:

  • They write small and often. A few hundred words a day, most days, beats a heroic weekend once a month. Consistency compounds; bursts do not.
  • They externalize the book. Notes, character details, and timelines live somewhere they can see, not in memory they have to refresh.
  • They get read before they are done. A slow chapter found in a draft is a fix. The same chapter found by an agent is a rejection.

What this means for your draft

If you have a half-finished book sitting in a folder, the problem is almost never the writing. It is that opening the file costs too much. The way back in is to lower that cost: keep the book's memory outside your head, write a little every day, and read your own pages like a stranger would.

That is the whole idea behind Slima. Not to write the book for you, but to carry the parts of it that were never meant to live in working memory, so the next session starts with the page instead of the recap.
