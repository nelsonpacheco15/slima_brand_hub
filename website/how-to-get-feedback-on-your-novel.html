You finished the draft. You hand it to your partner, your best friend, your mum. A week later you get it back: "I loved it, dear." And you have learned absolutely nothing.

This is the quiet problem with getting feedback on your novel. The people who love you don't want to hurt you, so they tell you it's wonderful. The strangers who promised to read it go quiet and ghost you. And the few honest readers you do find take three months to get back to you — by which point you've already started rewriting the wrong thing.

Honest feedback is the single most useful thing a draft can receive, and it's the hardest thing for most writers to actually get. This guide walks through how to get feedback on your novel that you can use: who to ask, what to ask them, when to ask, and how to get an honest first read fast so the human feedback lands on a stronger draft.

1. Get clear on what feedback you actually need

Before you ask anyone for anything, decide what kind of feedback the draft is ready for. The most common mistake is asking for the wrong layer too early — getting line-level nitpicks on a draft whose third act doesn't work yet.

Developmental feedback

This is the big-picture read: does the story hold together? Is the pacing right? Do the characters earn their arcs? Does the middle sag? Developmental feedback is what a developmental editor charges good money for, and it's what you want first, while there's still room to change things.

Line and copy feedback

This is sentence-level: prose, rhythm, word choice, typos. It matters — but it's wasted effort on a chapter you might cut. Don't polish prose that may not survive the next structural pass.

If you're not sure the draft is even done enough to share, that's its own signal. Our guide on how to finish writing a book covers getting to a real, complete draft first.

2. Where to find beta readers and critique partners

The best feedback usually comes from people who aren't related to you. Beta readers read your finished draft as readers, telling you how the story lands. Critique partners are fellow writers you trade work with, often chapter by chapter, who can speak to craft.

  • Genre communities. Find readers who already love what you write. A literary reader and a thriller reader want completely different things; matching genre matters more than matching taste.
  • Critique swaps. The most reliable arrangement: you read theirs, they read yours. Reciprocity creates accountability that one-sided favors never do.
  • Writing groups and forums. Online and local groups are full of writers looking for the same thing you are. Be the kind of reader you'd want, and good partners find you.
  • Social media and newsletters. A simple call for beta readers in the right community often turns up more volunteers than you expect — though not all will follow through.

3. The questions that get honest answers

"What did you think?" invites "I loved it." Specific questions invite the truth. The goal is to lower the social cost of being honest — and to ask about behavior, not opinion.

  • Where did you get bored? Be explicit that you want to know. Most readers won't volunteer it unless you make it safe.
  • Where did you stop reading? If they put the book down somewhere, that spot is gold. The point where attention dies is the point that needs the most work.
  • Who did you stop caring about, and when? Character feedback is more useful framed as a moment than a verdict.
  • What did you skim? Skimming is honest in a way praise never is. Ask for it directly.

Notice the pattern: the most useful questions are about where readers lean in and where they stop reading. Those two signals tell you more than any star rating.

4. Why timing matters — don't query agents too soon

Get a real read on a complete draft, and get it before you start querying agents. This is the timing mistake that costs writers the most: the manuscript goes out into the world having never been read end to end by anyone but its author.

Agents see a lot of openings. A first chapter that hasn't been pressure-tested by an honest reader is easy to spot, and you only get one shot at each agent's inbox. Feedback first, querying second — in that order, always.

5. The ghosting and waiting problem

Here's the part nobody warns you about. You find three willing beta readers. One reads it in a weekend and gives you two sentences. One disappears entirely. One promises feedback "soon" for four months. Meanwhile your draft sits frozen, because you're afraid to revise the wrong thing without knowing what's actually broken.

Human feedback is irreplaceable, but it's slow, uneven, and hard to schedule around. The waiting is real, and it kills momentum. The fix isn't to stop asking humans — it's to stop making them your first read.

FAQ

Questions, answered plainly.

How do I find beta readers?–

Start where writers already gather: genre communities, online forums, critique swaps, local writing groups, and social media. The most reliable beta readers come from reciprocal swaps, where you read someone else's manuscript in exchange for a read of yours. Look for readers who enjoy your genre, set a clear deadline, and give them specific questions to answer rather than a vague request to tell you what they think.

What is an AI beta reader?+

An AI beta reader reads your whole novel and reports back like a thoughtful reader would: where the story pulled them in, where their attention dipped, and where they would have stopped reading. Slima's AI Beta Readers return that read in under a day, including an attention curve and the one thing to fix first. It is a fast, honest first pass that complements human beta readers and editors rather than replacing them.

When should I get feedback on my novel?+

Get a real read once you have a complete draft you are no longer actively rewriting, and well before you start querying agents. Feedback on a finished draft tells you whether the story holds together; feedback during the messy middle usually just tells you it is unfinished. Querying before anyone has read the book end to end is the most common avoidable mistake.

Is AI feedback as good as a developmental editor?+

No, and it is not meant to be. A developmental editor brings human judgment, taste, and a working relationship that AI cannot match. AI feedback is a fast, affordable first pass: it shows you where attention dips and what to fix first, so the read you eventually pay a human editor for starts from a stronger draft. Use it before human readers, not instead of them.
